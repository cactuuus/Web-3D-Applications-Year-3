History

The AeroPress was invented by Alan Adler, a retired Stanford engineering lecturer already known for inventing the Aerobie flying ring.
Adler was dissatisfied with the current brewing methods available (espresso machines, pour-over, and French press) and sought to create a new kind of brewer that would allow greater control over brewing parameters, such as brew time, water temperature, and grind size, to produce a better cup of coffee. In 2004, he began prototyping in his garage, and a year later, in 2005, the AeroPress was launched.

Brewing tip: You can brew a stronger, concentrated coffee, then dilute it with hot water afterwards. This technique is known as bypass, allowing you to push beyond the AeroPress capacity, and adjust the strength of your brew to your liking.

A world-class brewer

The AeroPress revealed to be a huge success, gaining a cult following among coffee enthusiasts. Much of this is a result of the brewer's versatility and consistency, paired with a very low cost, durable design, and relatively simple brewing process (that doesn't require a lot of specialized equipment or knowledge).
This versatility pushed the creativity of the coffee community, which started experimenting and developing new techniques, such as the inverted technique, which (as the name implies) involves brewing with the AeroPress upside down, allowing for a longer steep time and even more control over the brewing process.
In 2008, the World AeroPress Championship was born, originally a small competition between friends, which eventually grew into a global event, living up to its name. This annual competition attracts baristas and coffee enthusiasts from around the world, who compete to create the best cup of coffee using the AeroPress, showcasing the brewer's versatility and the creativity of its users. Winning recipes are then shared online, for anyone to try!
