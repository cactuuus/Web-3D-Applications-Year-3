History

In 1941, German chemist Dr. Peter Schlumbohm applied his laboratory expertise to the domestic kitchen, seeking to create "the perfect cup" through pure chemistry. Drawing inspiration from the Bauhaus movement and laboratory glassware (specifically a modified Erlenmeyer flask and a glass funnel) he designed the Chemex as a single piece of non-porous borosilicate glass.
Beyond the lab, the Chemex became a visual shorthand for mid-century sophistication. While it holds a permanent place in the MoMA, its true cultural legacy is its status as a silent background icon in film and television. From the kitchen of Don Draper in Mad Men to the countertops of Monica Geller in Friends, it signals a specific blend of scientific precision and domestic elegance.

Delicate brewing

The Chemex is a percolation brewer, meaning extraction occurs as water passes through a bed of coffee grounds and a filter by only using gravity. Its defining characteristic is the use of thicker paper filters, featuring a much tighter fiber mesh.
This dense mesh acts traps nearly all sediment and most natural oils. Because the water navigates this thick barrier slowly, it creates a steady percolation that yields a brew with unparalleled clarity and a tea-like body, highlighting the delicate top notes of the bean.

Brewing tip

Always rinse the heavy paper filter with hot water before adding coffee. This will help reduce channelling, and remove any papery taste from the brew.
