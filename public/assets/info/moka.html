History

In 1933, Italian engineer Alfonso Bialetti completed the design for the Moka Express, a project born from observing the operation of a primitive laundry machine that used a heat-driven tube to disperse soapy water. By miniaturizing the massive, high-pressure steam technology previously found only in commercial coffee houses, Bialetti effectively democratized "espresso-style" coffee for the domestic kitchen. After WWII, the brewer became a global icon of Made in Italy design, largely fueled by the marketing of Bialetti's son, Renato, who introduced the "L'Omino con i baffi" mascot.
Today, the Moka remains an industrial masterpiece, held in the permanent collections of the MoMA and the Science Museum in London as a testament to the "Italian Economic Miracle."

How it works

The Moka pot is a three-chambered system that utilizes basic thermodynamics to achieve extraction.
Water in the bottom Boiler is heated until it produces steam. As this steam expands, it creates pressure that pushes the remaining hot water up through a funnel and into the filter basket. Here, the water saturates the coffee grounds under approximately 1.5 bars of pressure. The brewed coffee then travels through a second filter and up a central delivery tube, finally "erupting" into the top Collector.
This process results in a high-extraction brew with a concentration and flavor profile sitting somewhere between traditional drip coffee and modern espresso.

Brewing tip

For better results, once the coffee starts to flow into the top chamber, try to keep the flow steady by removing the pot from the heat, then returning it once the flow slows down. This helps in achieving a more even and gentle extraction.
